Look closely at a single candle and you're looking at a compressed little history of everything buyers and sellers did to each other over that stretch of time. The open tells you where the argument started, the close tells you who was left standing, and the wicks show you the ground that got fought over and lost. Once you can read that shape properly, the name attached to it stops mattering very much. The names are still worth having, though — if only so you're not at sea when someone mentions a "shooting star" on a chart.

---

## Single-candle patterns

**Doji**

{{< figure src="doji.png" caption="A doji — open and close pinned to almost the same price, wicks either side." >}}

Here, the open and the close land on almost exactly the same price, however far the wicks reach out on either side. Nobody won this round — it was a stalemate. Taken on its own, that's really all a doji tells you: indecision, nothing more specific than that. Where it gets interesting is context. I mostly only pay attention to a doji when it turns up after a strong run, or sitting right at a level I was already watching. Buried in the middle of a quiet range, it's just noise.

*Example: EURUSD grinds higher through the New York session, prints a doji right at the day's high, then rolls over the next morning — that's the kind worth marking. A doji in the middle of an Asian-session chop isn't telling you anything.*

**Pin bar (hammer / shooting star)**

{{< figure src="pin-bar.png" caption="Bullish pin bar (left) and bearish pin bar (right) — the long wick is the rejection, the close confirms it." >}}

A long wick in one direction, a small body closing away from it. A long lower wick with the close up near the top is the bullish version — price was shoved down hard and then thrown straight back. A long upper wick with the close down near the bottom is the same idea in reverse. It's the same shape wearing two different names depending on where it turns up: a "hammer" after a downtrend, a "shooting star" after an uptrend. The wick records the test; the close tells you who actually won it.

*Example: GER40 spikes down into the Frankfurt Open low, wicks well below it, then closes back near the top of the candle. That's a bullish pin at a level worth reacting to — the same shape sitting in open air mid-session isn't.*

**Marubozu**

{{< figure src="marubozu.png" caption="Bullish marubozu (left) and bearish marubozu (right) — full commitment, no wick either end." >}}

Barely a wick at either end — the open sits close to the high (or the low), and the close sits close to the low (or the high), for the whole period. One side had full command of the tape from start to finish. It carries more weight breaking through a level than it does drifting through open space with nothing around it.

*Example: a bearish marubozu closing straight through the Overnight Low with no wick — that's momentum actually clearing the level, not just testing it.*

## Two-candle patterns

**Engulfing**

{{< figure src="engulfing.png" caption="Bullish engulfing (left pair) and bearish engulfing (right pair) — the second candle swallows the first." >}}

The second candle's body swallows the first one whole — opens beyond it, closes beyond it too. The bullish version follows a down move and closes back above where the previous candle opened: buyers didn't just stall the slide, they took the whole thing back with size behind them. The bearish version is the same story told in reverse. It means rather more when that first candle was itself part of the existing move, because then the reversal has actually absorbed some real momentum, rather than just swallowing a quiet pullback.

*Example: a sharp down candle into support, followed by a candle that engulfs it and closes above the down candle's open — that's a reversal with something behind it, worth more attention than an engulfing candle that just appears mid-range.*

**Tweezer tops and bottoms**

{{< figure src="tweezer.png" caption="Tweezer top (left) and tweezer bottom (right) — the same price tested and rejected twice." >}}

Two candles sharing close to the same high or low, with bodies pointing in opposite directions. The same price got tested twice and rejected twice — which gives you a cheap, quick way to see a level being defended without needing to wait for a full swing to confirm it.

*Example: two consecutive candles both wicking into the same supply zone and both closing lower — the level's being defended, even without a clean swing high to point at.*

**Inside bar**

{{< figure src="inside-bar.png" caption="An inside bar — its entire range sits inside the candle before it." >}}

Here the whole candle — high and low both — sits tucked inside the range of the one before it. Nothing about direction; it's purely a question of range. Think of it as the market pausing to catch its breath after a move, coiling rather than committing. On its own it's just compression. What follows the coil is where the story gets interesting — which is exactly what the three-candle pattern below builds on.

*Example: a strong push into a level, then a small candle that trades entirely within the previous one's range before the session goes quiet — that's the coil worth watching for the break.*

**Outside bar**

{{< figure src="outside-bar.png" caption="Bullish outside bar (left) and bearish outside bar (right) — the whole range engulfs the candle before it, not just the body." >}}

The mirror image of an inside bar: this candle's range — high and low both — completely swallows the one before it. Don't confuse this with an engulfing candle above, which is only ever about the bodies. An outside bar is about the full range, wicks included, which makes it a genuine jump in participation rather than just a change of mind. Everyone who was sitting on the sidelines during the previous candle showed up in this one.

*Example: a quiet, narrow-range candle followed by one that trades well above and well below it, closing strongly in one direction — that's a real shift in who's turned up to trade, not just a bigger body.*

## Three-candle patterns

**Morning star / evening star**

{{< figure src="morning-evening-star.png" caption="Morning star (left) and evening star (right) — the middle candle is where the move loses its nerve." >}}

A strong candle in the direction of the trend, then a small-bodied candle — often a doji — that gaps or drifts away from it, then a strong candle back the other way that closes past the midpoint of the first. That middle candle is the hesitation: the exact point where the move runs out of conviction before it turns. Morning star at the bottom of a move, evening star at the top.

*Example: a strong down candle, a small doji that barely moves, then a strong up candle closing well into the first candle's range — that sequence at a known demand zone is a lot more interesting than the same three candles sitting in the middle of nowhere.*

**Three inside up / down**

{{< figure src="three-inside.png" caption="Three inside up (left) and three inside down (right) — compression, then a real break." >}}

The inside bar from above forms after a directional candle, then the third candle breaks its range in the other direction entirely — the coil, followed by the break. It's the same idea as the inside-bar setups in [Fair Value Gaps](/learn/market-structure/fair-value-gaps/) — compression after a move, followed by a genuine break.

*Example: a down move into a level, an inside bar sitting quietly inside the previous candle's range, then a candle that breaks back up through both — that's three inside up, and it's the same "compression, then break" idea you'll see again in the FVG setups.*

## What actually matters

None of this works terribly well in isolation, and that's where most candlestick guides come unstuck — treating the shape itself as the whole story. A bullish engulfing candle sitting in the middle of an unremarkable range doesn't tell you a great deal. The same candle at a support level, a session open, or a demand zone is an entirely different proposition, because the level is what gives the reaction its meaning in the first place.

So look at the level and the wider picture before you look at the candle. The candle is only ever telling you whether the reaction is actually turning up.

---

## Key takeaways

- A candlestick pattern is confirmation of what's happening at a level, not a signal to trade off on its own
- Doji = indecision, only worth noting after a strong move or at a level you're already watching
- Pin bar (hammer/shooting star) = rejection shown by the wick, confirmed by where it closes
- Engulfing candles carry more weight when the first candle was part of the existing trend
- Tweezers show a level defended twice without needing a full swing to confirm it
- Inside bar = compression, the whole range sits inside the candle before it — a coil, not a direction
- Outside bar = a jump in participation, the whole range (not just the body) swallows the candle before it
- Morning/evening star = a three-candle pause-then-reverse at the end of a move
- Check the level first — the pattern only means something in context
