A candlestick pattern isn't a signal on its own — it's a snapshot of who won a fight between buyers and sellers over a given period. Learn to read what the shape is telling you, and the name becomes secondary. That said, the names are a useful shorthand, so here's what's worth actually knowing.

---

## Single-candle patterns

**Doji**
Open and close are essentially the same price, regardless of how far the wicks extend either way. Neither side won. On its own it means indecision — nothing more. At the end of a strong trend, into a key level, it's worth paying attention to. In the middle of a range, it's noise.

**Pin bar (hammer / shooting star)**
A long wick in one direction with a small body closing away from it. A long lower wick with a close near the top is a bullish pin — price was pushed down hard and rejected. A long upper wick with a close near the bottom is a bearish pin — same idea, inverted. Same shape, two names, depending on where it shows up: "hammer" after a downtrend, "shooting star" after an uptrend. The wick is the rejection. The body is the confirmation of which side actually won.

**Marubozu**
A candle with little to no wick on either end — the open is the high (or low) and the close is the low (or high). Full commitment for the entire period, no pullback either way. Strongest when it shows up breaking a level rather than drifting in open air.

---

## Two-candle patterns

**Engulfing**
The second candle's body fully engulfs the body of the first, closing beyond both its open and close. A bullish engulfing follows a down move and closes strongly above the prior candle's open — buyers didn't just stop the slide, they took it back with size. Bearish is the mirror image. The engulf matters more when the first candle was itself a continuation of the existing move — it means the reversal absorbed real momentum, not just a quiet pullback.

**Tweezer tops and bottoms**
Two candles sharing (almost) the same high or low, with opposite-direction bodies. It shows the same price was tested twice and rejected twice — a cheap way to spot a level being defended without needing a full swing structure to confirm it.

---

## Three-candle patterns

**Morning star / evening star**
A strong candle in the direction of the trend, followed by a small-bodied candle (often a doji) that gaps or sits away from it, followed by a strong candle in the opposite direction closing back past the midpoint of the first. The middle candle is the pause — the moment the trend loses conviction before it turns. Morning star at the bottom of a move, evening star at the top.

**Three inside up / down**
An inside bar forms after a directional candle, then the third candle breaks the inside bar's range in the opposite direction to the original move. Same logic as the inside-bar setups covered in [Fair Value Gaps](/learn/market-structure/fair-value-gaps/) — compression after a move, then a decisive break.

---

## What actually matters

None of these patterns work in isolation, and treating a shape as a signal by itself is how most candlestick guides go wrong. A bullish engulfing candle in the middle of a range means very little. The same candle at a support level, a session open, or a supply/demand zone is a different trade entirely — the pattern is confirmation that a level is holding, not a reason to trade on its own.

Context first, pattern second: what's the level, what's the higher timeframe doing, is there anything else pointing at the same area. The candle just tells you whether the reaction is actually happening.

---

## Key takeaways

- A candlestick pattern describes who won a period, not a standalone signal — treat it as confirmation, not a trigger
- Doji = indecision; only meaningful at the end of a move or at a key level
- Pin bar (hammer/shooting star) = long wick rejection, small body showing which side won
- Engulfing candles matter more when the first candle was part of the existing trend
- Tweezers show a level defended twice without needing a full swing to confirm it
- Morning/evening star = a three-candle pause-and-reverse at the end of a move
- Always read the pattern against the level or zone it forms at — context first, pattern second
